## Marking Criteria

| Weight | Criterion | Sub-criteria |
|---:|---|---|
| 20% | Knowledge and Understanding | Subject knowledge and project motivation; literature review and state of the art; understanding of methodologies, techniques, and tools. |
| 20% | Critical Thinking | Justification of research question; justification of methods and techniques; interpretation and evaluation of results; critical evaluation of own solution or approach. |
| 20% | Technical Quality and Technical Achievements | Soundness and correctness of methods; technical correctness of results; completeness against aims and objectives; quality of experimental design, software, or theoretical development. |
| 20% | Originality | Novelty of research question; innovativeness of approach, method, or software; significance of contribution; amount of contribution and work done. |
| 15% | Presentation Quality | Structure; clarity of sentences and expressions; spelling and grammar; formatting, academic style, and appropriate length; usefulness of visual elements; correctness of citations and references. |
| 5% | Professionalism | Project, time, and risk management; ability to self-manage. |

## Score Bands

| Score band | Expected standard |
|---|---|
| 0 | No meaningful evidence for the criterion: no knowledge, no literature review, no method, no result, no contribution, no useful visual elements, or failed project/self-management. |
| 1%-29% | Very limited and superficial work. Important related work is mostly missing, methods or results may contain large mistakes, contribution and work volume are insufficient, and the project relies heavily on supervisor direction. |
| 30%-39% | Below pass level. The rubric leaves many cells blank in this band, but work at this level is generally incomplete, weakly justified, and technically or presentationally insufficient. |
| 40%-49% | Some relevant work is present, but coverage, explanation, justification, and evaluation are partial. Some aims are achieved, but methods, results, or writing may still be unclear or incomplete. |
| 50%-59% | Basic master dissertation standard. Knowledge, formatting, and writing are generally adequate; methods are broadly sound; results have at most small mistakes; the work achieves basic aims and contribution requirements. |
| 60%-69% | Good standard. Most relevant literature, methods, and tools are explained clearly; most main results are evaluated systematically; most objectives are achieved; the work is generally complete and could be extended toward publishable quality. |
| 70%-79% | Excellent standard. Justifications are evidence-based and convincing; methods and results are technically correct; all objectives are achieved; contribution and workload exceed basic requirements; structure, references, and visual elements are strong. |
| 80%-89% | Very high standard. Results are evaluated completely and in depth; experimental design, software, or theoretical development is professional and high quality; the work has potential for publication in reputable venues. |
| 90%-100% | Outstanding standard. The dissertation shows deep and systematic understanding, comprehensive state-of-the-art discussion, fully justified methodology, technically verified results, in-depth wider-context discussion, strong original contribution, and excellent self-management. |

## High-Priority Checklist

- State the research aims, objectives, and research question clearly.
- Explain the project context and motivation, including why the problem matters.
- Provide a systematic literature review and connect key prior work to this project.
- Justify the chosen methods with evidence from literature or technical reasoning.
- Demonstrate that methods and results are technically correct.
- Evaluate results thoroughly, including limitations and wider context.
- Critically evaluate the proposed solution, approach, software, or method.
- Make the original contribution explicit and explain its significance.
- Present experiments, software, or theoretical development professionally.
- Keep structure, formatting, academic style, grammar, figures, tables, citations, and references polished.
- Show evidence of steady project management, risk management, and self-management.
